01 Phase 1: The Promise

Reflections on Corporate Sustainability Claims and Lobbying

When I proposed this research, I wasn't only thinking about how to graduate, but I was thinking about what research I could do that would genuinely interest me. I was initially considering going down the more sciency route, looking at specific numbers, charts, benchmarks, and everything else. It was when I was coming up with my initial plan that I changed my mind, as I realised just how insane some of the claims were, and that they would have a real impact on people if they weren't lived up to.

Reading that companies like Meta, Google, and Amazon wanted to be close to net-zero, carbon free, or even just "way more sustainable than we were before", set off alarm bells, as I knew that doing this would likely be super expensive, and the economic system in the US doesn't reward "wasting money" on things that won't increase shareholder value. It therefore made lots of sense when I saw that lobbying was rampant during the proposals for some of these projects, and there are likely even more under the table deals that weren't exposed by whistleblowers or third-party organizations. As someone who uses AI daily, and has seen a real productivity boost from tools like Claude Code, Gemini, and other products made by these companies, the irony wasn't lost on me when I started to write about how companies lie about these things, and how our usage of these tools only fuels their need to take advantage of our infrastructure.

Seeing the scale of the lies vs reality was also interesting, and I learned a lot about how lobbying works on a fundamental level by reading through some of these cases.

02 Phase 1.5: The Labor

Reflections on the Invisible Workforce Behind AI

After talking about how the footprint of AI infra actually goes far deeper than just building datacenters, I realized that I had the perfect experience to include an unplanned addendum about some companies that I had less than positive experiences with. It also gave me a chance to learn about how they were affecting those in countries with far lower wages than here. I was reached out to for a Remotasks role almost 3 years ago, and I was promised over 50 USD an hour, and a place on their exclusive "elite coder" team. Turns out that I wasn't the only one who got this exact same reachout, and it looks like this was an intentional bot reaching out to capitalize on college students who are more likely to take up freelancing work as they aren't working full time. Scale AI, the parent company, relies on over 240k workers from Kenya, Philippines, Venezuela and other nations where minimum wages are low, and workers' rights are essentially nonexistent.

I didn't even consider this as I never worked on content moderation, or with sensitive topics, but the psychological impact of reading about some of the most depraved possible generated content must have been terrible for everyone involved, especially once you add in the fact that people weren't getting paid on time, accounts were getting banned for no reason, and general sentiment in the private channels always seemed low. I hated the assessments when I signed up, and they really did seem like a chance for Scale AI to siphon free labor from us, while they dangled a 500 dollar bonus ahead of us to keep us going.

It didn't surprise me to learn that they had been sued multiple times due to workers' rights.

03 Phase 2: The Price

Reflections on Community Impact Case Studies

I felt like I had become numb to what these companies were doing on a wider scale, but reading about case-level studies really made me think about how real individuals were losing not only their clean air, but also basic drinking water, all while being priced out of the places they called home. The scale of corruption that allowed this to happen to them was truly shocking, and reading about local governments that were meant to fight for their citizens bending over backwards at the sound of "commitments" was pretty disappointing. In Richland Parish, people talked about how they were being evicted, not just from their suburbs, but even lower cost trailer parks, all so that construction workers could move in to build the datacenters that would slowly kill them in multiple ways.

In my opinion, the situation in Mexico was even worse, with 19 out of the 21 projects being incomplete. The fact that fields were left open and bare without the development they promised, just goes to show how these companies know they can get away with anything in these places, as all potential legal ramifications are cancelled out by hush money, or other personal privileges afforded to any officials who work with the lobbyists.

This seems to follow a theme that I've seen when I do research about ethics in other parts of engineering, where the pains are public, but the pleasures are privatized, and only those who were never at risk receive the reward.
