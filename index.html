Phase 1 — The Corporate Promise & The Industry Landscape

The Illusion of Clean AI

How Big Tech sells a green future while lobbying to prevent it

February 2026 · 18 min read

Most big tech companies have sustainability pages hidden somewhere under all the news about their latest innovations. They all promise the same thing about carbon negative, water positive, net-zero timelines, but never follow up on these commitments. Whenever people ask public-facing teams, they always seem to restate the same points.

But when you look deeper behind the scenes, you can see how their real corporate interests are much different. There are cases of ignoring water limits in drought-stricken towns, generators built without permission in lower income towns, and many other instances that suggest that the words and actions of big tech don't always align.

My goal for this first phase of my research was to lay out in clear terms what these companies say they'll do, and then show what they're actually doing.

Part I: The Promises

I started off my research by going through some ESG reports and sustainability pages for four hyperscalers building the datacenters (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) and three semiconductor companies designing the chips inside them (NVIDIA, AMD, Intel). It was interesting to see that they all used the same vocabulary and the exact same underlying message of: we acknowledge the problem, we're investing in solutions, and our technology is actually part of the answer.

Here's what they're promising:

Microsoft

Carbon negative by 2030. Water positive by 2030. Remove all historical emissions by 2050.

Microsoft claims to have contracted 34 GW of carbon-free energy across 24 countries and secured over 30 million metric tons in carbon removal deals. Their CSO, Melanie Nakagawa, frames AI itself as the solution: the same force pushing them away from their goals in the short term will build the "bigger, faster rocket" to reach them long term.1

Google

Net-zero across all operations and value chain by 2030. 24/7 carbon-free energy on every grid.

Google reports a fleet-wide datacenter PUE (Power Usage Effectiveness) of 1.09 — extremely efficient by industry standards — and claims its products enabled 26 million metric tons of CO₂e reductions in 2024 through tools like fuel-efficient routing in Maps and the Nest thermostat. The new Ironwood TPU is marketed as nearly 30x more power efficient than Google's first Cloud TPU from 2018.2

Amazon / AWS

Net-zero by 2040 via The Climate Pledge. 100% renewable energy match for a second year.

Amazon co-founded The Climate Pledge in 2019 and claims to match all electricity with renewables across 621 projects globally (34 GW total). AWS markets itself as 4.1x more energy efficient than running your own servers on-premises.3

Meta

Net-zero in global operations since 2020. Net-zero across entire value chain by 2030.

All of Meta's owned datacenters are certified LEED Gold or higher (52 buildings, roughly 36 million square feet). Meta reports returning 1.59 billion gallons of water to high-stress regions and even built a $70 million water treatment facility in Kuna, Idaho, then gifted it to the city.4

NVIDIA

Blackwell GPUs: 25x more energy efficient than Hopper for large LLMs. 50x more than CPUs for inference.

NVIDIA's FY2025 sustainability report claims that switching HPC and AI workloads from CPU-only servers to GPU-accelerated systems could save 40 trillion watt-hours annually.5

AMD

Exceeded "30x25" energy efficiency goal at 38.5x. Targets 95% power reduction in AI training racks by 2030.

AMD claims its next efficiency target would reduce the number of server racks needed for AI training from 275 to less than one.6

Intel

Net-zero Scope 1 and 2 by 2040. Net positive water globally by 2030.

Intel has already achieved water positivity in the U.S., India, Costa Rica, and Mexico.7

Reading these reports back-to-back, it would be easy for anyone to assume that these companies are actively working with the best interests of society at heart. I think this is because they make public promises but silent admissions, and the numbers they in their own filings seem to agree.

Part II: The Silent Discrepancies

The strongest evidence against these pledges comes from the companies' own sustainability reports. Below are just some of the highlights I found when looking at things that are publicly available, albeit far less visible than the promises.

48%
Google's emissions increase since 2019 — reaching 14.3 million metric tons CO₂e in 2023
Google 2024 Environmental Report

Google quietly abandoned its prior claim of operational carbon neutrality and now acknowledges that net-zero by 2030 is "extremely ambitious." This is essentially a more eco-friendly way of saying "We lied, but we definitely tried."

23.4%
Microsoft's emissions increase from its 2020 baseline — to roughly 14.86 million metric tons in FY2024
Microsoft 2024 Sustainability Report

Microsoft's electricity consumption nearly tripled between 2020 and 2024 (10.8 million MWh to 29.8 million MWh). Water use rose 87% since 2020. Their Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) commitment was removed in spring 2024 after they failed to submit a compliant plan. Microsoft's President said that they would now need to "speed up the work needed to make it more environmentally friendly.", rather than address the failures that led to these alarming increases.

68.25M
Amazon's absolute emissions in metric tons for 2024 — up 6% year-over-year, roughly 20M tons above 2019 baseline
Stand.earth analysis

Amazon was removed from SBTi entirely for failing to file reduction targets. Meanwhile, Meta's Scope 3 emissions rose by about 1 million tonnes in 2024 alone, making its 2031 target of not exceeding the 2021 baseline look increasingly unreachable.

The 662% Problem

A September 2024 Guardian investigation found that actual datacenter emissions from Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Apple are likely 662% higher than officially reported. This is because companies use Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) — essentially paper credits — that don't require the renewable energy to actually be consumed at their facilities. Under location-based accounting (physical grid activity), Meta's 2022 Scope 2 emissions jump from 273 metric tons to over 3.8 million metric tons. Microsoft's jump from 280,782 to 6.1 million.8

This is serious enough that 16 state attorneys general launched a formal investigation in September 2025, calling these accounting practices "environmental accounting gimmicks" and a "shell game."9

The NewClimate Institute's 2025 Corporate Climate Responsibility Monitor summarized this well by saying that GHG emissions targets for tech companies appear to have "lost their meaning and relevance."

Part III: The Lobbying Disconnect

While the sustainability reports are published on their website, the amount of money spent on lobbying against the very numbers they pledged are much more well hidden.

$61.5M
Combined Big Tech federal lobbying spending in 2024 — a 13% increase from 2023
Issue One

From 2020 to 2024, these companies spent a combined $260 million on federal lobbying. Meta alone employed nearly 300 lobbyists in 2024, which is around one for every other member of Congress. They hit a record $26.29 million in spending in 2025. NVIDIA's lobbying surged 7x, from $350,000 in 2023 to $4.9 million in 2025, as AI regulation discussions intensified.10

Looking at what these tech giants spend the money lobbying against helps to paint a picture of what they value enough to commit to funding, versus words at a press conference. A 2021 InfluenceMap analysis found that just 4% of Big Tech's federal lobbying activities focused on climate-related policy. Senator Sheldon Whitehouse put it bluntly when he said that Big Tech has "refused to lift a finger to push comprehensive climate action in Congress."11

At the state level, there are many clear examples of how companies have overruled the interests of the people that live there:

Virginia

The world's largest datacenter market. The Data Center Coalition (members include Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Meta) lobbied against zoning restrictions and environmental protections. All 17 bills aimed at tighter datacenter regulation were defeated or postponed in the 2026 legislative session. The state spent $732 million on its datacenter sales tax exemption in FY2024, which now costs $1.6 billion annually.12

Louisiana

The state rewrote zoning laws and suspended competitive bidding requirements to accommodate Meta's $10 billion Hyperion datacenter. Entergy is building three new natural gas-fired power plants to serve them, which will consume roughly twice the energy used by New Orleans on a peak day. Regular ratepayers may have to pay for up to half the cost of the new power infrastructure in bill increases.13

Ohio

The state granted $2.5 billion in tax incentives between 2017 and 2024. Thirteen approved agreements covering $5.1 billion in investment generated only 356 jobs — that's a revenue loss of nearly $1 million per job created. Amazon's exemption alone potentially lasts through 2055.14

Good Jobs First, a subsidy watchdog organization, states: "We know of no other form of state spending that is so out of control… such subsidies are absolutely unnecessary for an extremely profitable industry dominated by some of the most valuable corporations on earth." These companies collectively plan $320–$400 billion in capital expenditure in 2025 alone, projected to reach $650–$700 billion in 2026 — yet they extract billions in tax exemptions from communities with poverty rates exceeding 25%.15

Part IV: Water, Energy, and the Physical Reality

Behind the accounting tricks and lobbying dollars are real resources being consumed in real places.

6.4B gal
Google's global water consumption in 2023 (95% for datacenters) — up 17% from 2022
Google Environmental Report

A single Google facility in Council Bluffs, Iowa, consumed roughly 1 billion gallons, which is enough to supply all of Iowa's residential water for five days. Microsoft's water use rose 87% since 2020. U.S. datacenters collectively consumed 17 billion gallons directly through cooling in 2023, with an additional 211 billion gallons consumed indirectly through electricity generation. The Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory projects this could double or quadruple by 2028.16

These demands hit hardest in the driest places. Google's Mesa, Arizona datacenter holds a water permit for 5.5 million cubic meters per year at a negotiated rate of $6.08 per 1,000 gallons while residents pay $10.80. Google is expanding with a second Mesa datacenter despite Arizona revoking construction permits for new homes in Maricopa County in June 2023 due to groundwater depletion. A former Amazon water sustainability manager, Nathan Wangusi, publicly stated: "I raised the issue in all the right places that this is not ethical."17

415 TWh
Global datacenter electricity consumption in 2024 - projected to reach 945 TWh by 2030 (equivalent to Japan's total usage)
IEA Energy and AI Report

A single ChatGPT query uses roughly 10x the electricity of a Google search. Training GPT-4 is estimated at 51,773–62,319 MWh, over 40x what GPT-3 required. More than half of U.S. datacenter electricity comes from fossil fuels. In Georgia, regulators approved roughly 10,000 MW of new capacity including five new natural gas plants specifically to serve datacenters — placing what the Southern Environmental Law Center called "a $60 billion risk on the backs of everyday Georgians."18

Part V: The "AI Will Fix It" Defense

The industry's go-to response to all of this is that AI will ultimately generate more environmental benefits than costs. While at this point we know not to take claims from these companies to heart, I still wanted to look into any potential truth that could be behind this statement.

A February 2026 report by Beyond Fossil Fuels, Stand.earth, and the Green Web Foundation analyzed 154 AI climate claims from Big Tech. Interestingly, only 26% cited published academic papers. A full 36% cited no evidence whatsoever. Most importantly, researchers found zero verified examples of consumer generative AI leading to material emissions reductions.19

The report identifies what it calls a "bait-and-switch": companies conflate "traditional AI" (which has documented climate applications and a lower energy footprint) with "generative AI" (which has a massive footprint and no verified climate benefits). When Google says its AI enabled 26 million tons of reductions, more than half of that claim is based on interviews without third-party validation, according to an Internet Policy Review analysis.20

"It appears tech companies are using vagueness about what happens within energy-hogging data centres to greenwash a planet-wrecking expansion." — Ketan Joshi, report author, Beyond Fossil Fuels (2026)

The Internet Policy Review characterized this as "obfuscation-by-complexity". This is slightly different from textbook greenwashing, where companies simply mislead consumers into thinking that their products are more environmentally friendly than they actually are. Enabled emissions are never reported while speculative avoided emissions are highlighted. Microsoft's electricity consumption nearly tripled in four years, but their sustainability reports emphasize declining "market-based" emissions.

Part VI: Communities Pay the Price

The abstract policy failures translate to concrete human impacts. As of 2025, $64 billion in U.S. datacenter projects have been blocked or delayed by community opposition, showing that real people have real grievances about how all these supposed "innovations" are actually hurting them.21

In Loudoun County, Virginia,home to roughly 200 operational datacenters and 117 more in the pipeline, water usage increased 250% between 2019 and 2023. The county ended by-right datacenter zoning in March 2025, now requiring public hearings. County Chair Phyllis Randall noted that now constituents say "I don't care.", before listening to whatever bribe these companies often stage at local meetings.22

The environmental justice side of things is equally concerning. In Memphis, xAI installed 35 unpermitted methane gas turbines in Boxtown, a predominantly Black community where cancer risk is already 4x the national average. University of Tennessee researchers found peak NO₂ levels increased 79% near the facility. The NAACP partnered with the Southern Environmental Law Center to challenge the installation, and the EPA ruled in January 2026 that xAI violated the Clean Air Act. Tennessee State Rep. Justin Pearson's remarked: "Our lives and our lungs are being sacrificed on the altar of their capitalistic exploitation."23

A national poll found only 44% of Americans would welcome a datacenter nearby. This makes datacenters less popular than gas plants, wind farms, or nuclear facilities. Public sentiment is clearly shifting away from these supposed "life-changing" technologies.

My Final Thoughts

Going into this research, I felt like I had a good idea on how companies would often overpromise and underdeliver. I knew they would always prioritize their shareholders, as is their legal obligation. What I didn't expect was how structural and intentional the disconnect is. It looks like there are three key ways they do this:

Accounting: REC-based "market-based" reporting allows companies to claim clean energy use while their physical emissions may be up to 662% higher than reported. The 16-state attorney general investigation suggests this isn't just an academic concern.

Lobbying: Trade associations funded by these companies systematically defeat the environmental regulations that would make their own pledges enforceable. Only 4% of their federal lobbying considers climate policy. A 2023 InfluenceMap study found that 58% of major companies with climate commitments had lobbying activities that directly contradicted those commitments.24

Distribution: Communities in Loudoun County, Mesa, Memphis, and rural Louisiana bear the water depletion, noise, diesel emissions, and grid strain. The companies that promised to think about the people extract billions in tax exemptions that drain the public services those communities need.

The gap between promise and action seems to be an intended feature of how the industry operates, and it clearly has real effects on people who unwillingly have these new establishments developed around them. The rest of my research will hopefully help uncover this, as I move away from company talk and look more at how communities are affected by and responding to this.

Next up: Phase 2 is where I plan to go into even more detail about how these companies are affecting individuals. My aim is to document how these dynamics play out in real peoples lives through their bills, drinking water, and community spaces.
