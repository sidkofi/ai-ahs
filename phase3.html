Community Responses & Global Resistance

The Pushback

How communities worldwide are fighting the unchecked expansion of AI infrastructure

March 2026 · 14 min read

Phase 1 documented the gap between Big Tech's sustainability promises and their actual emissions, water use, and lobbying. Phase 2 looked at what this means on the ground in two communities. For Phase 3, I wanted to answer a different question: what are people doing about it?

The answer, it turns out, is quite a lot. From Virginia subdivisions to Chilean environmental courts to Irish energy regulators, a global movement is forming to push back against the unchecked expansion of data centers. It is not a single coordinated campaign. It is hundreds of local fights that are increasingly learning from one another.

What's Working

Looking across these cases, a few strategies have proven effective at either stopping projects outright or forcing meaningful concessions from developers.

Grassroots Organizing at Public Hearings

The most common and often most effective tactic is simply showing up. In Indianapolis, hundreds of residents packed a City-County Council meeting in September 2025, requiring two overflow rooms. Google withdrew its 468-acre rezoning request minutes before the vote, which was expected to fail. In Louisa County, Virginia, sustained community pressure led Amazon Web Services to withdraw a 7.2 million square foot proposal in July 2025. In Tucson, Arizona, residents attended town board meetings, wrote op-eds, and emailed officials about "Project Blue," an Amazon-backed hyperscale facility that would have consumed millions of gallons of desert drinking water. The city council unanimously voted to discontinue discussions with the developer.

Legal and Regulatory Challenges

In Prince William County, Virginia, two lawsuits filed against data centers planned near the Manassas National Battlefield Park culminated in a court ruling that voided a major rezoning. The project now faces a mandated revote and a timeline that could stretch at least two years. In Chile, environmental courts revoked the license for Google's second data center in Santiago after community complaints about water consumption, forcing the company to commit to waterless cooling. In Memphis, Tennessee, the EPA ruled in January 2026 that xAI violated the Clean Air Act by installing 35 unpermitted methane gas turbines in a predominantly Black community.

National Moratoriums and Policy Shifts

Ireland imposed a de facto moratorium on new data center grid connections in Dublin from 2021 until late 2025. Data centers now consume nearly a quarter of Ireland's electricity. When the moratorium was finally lifted in December 2025, new rules required data centers to install on-site generation or battery systems matching their full demand, source 80% of electricity from new renewables, and feed excess generation back into the national grid. The Netherlands blocked Meta's proposed Zeewolde mega-campus after farmers challenged it on environmental and nitrogen-permit grounds. Singapore imposed its own three-year moratorium on new facilities. In the U.S., Georgia saw moratoriums enacted across eight municipalities, and New York proposed a statewide moratorium on data center permits in early 2026.

Community Benefit Agreements

Some communities have found ways to extract meaningful concessions rather than outright blocking projects. Lancaster, Pennsylvania established a model Community Benefit Agreement (CBA) for data center development that includes a water-use cap, noise and air emissions controls, and $20 million in community contributions. The Brookings Institution has advocated for CBAs as a way for communities to gain leverage, noting that the urgency of the AI buildout has put some localities "in the driver's seat" for deals. However, CBAs are only as strong as the enforcement mechanisms behind them, and the broken promises documented in Querétaro should serve as a warning about unenforceable commitments.

The Global South Fights Back

The resistance is not limited to wealthy nations. Across Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia, communities are pushing back against what researchers have called "digital extractivism."

In Chile, the activist organization Mosacat (Socio Environmental Community Movement for Land and Water) has staged sustained protests against Google, Microsoft, and Amazon data centers in Santiago. Years of activism by residents of Cerrillos forced Google to switch to less water-intensive cooling technology. In Uruguay, the Commission in Defense of Water and Life, the same coalition that led a 2004 campaign resulting in a constitutional amendment making clean water a human right, organized protests that forced Google to revise plans for its Canelones data center. In Mexico, the National Antorchista Movement has lobbied the Querétaro government to end water shortages and demand transparency about industry water consumption.

In Kenya, the High Court suspended Worldcoin's biometric data campaign and ordered the deletion of all collected iris scans, signaling growing judicial willingness to challenge tech companies operating without proper community consent. Across Africa, concerns about "digital colonialism" are driving calls for data sovereignty legislation that would require data to stay in-country and give communities a say in how infrastructure is built.

Malaysia moved to slow data center development in September 2024 after recognizing the strain on the nation's energy grid and water resources. These decisions reflect a growing understanding that the economic benefits promised by the data center industry are not automatic and that communities have the right to set terms.

What I Learned

The most important finding from this phase of research is that community opposition works. Not always, and not without significant effort, but the numbers are clear: 66% of projects facing sustained opposition are blocked or delayed. That is a powerful statistic for communities that feel powerless against trillion-dollar companies.

The second finding is that these fights are connected, even when the people involved do not know each other. A community in Indiana facing a Google data center is dealing with the same playbook, the same opacity, and the same set of promises as a farming community in Mexico dealing with Microsoft. The strategies that work in one place (showing up at hearings, filing environmental complaints, demanding water-use data) translate directly to others.

What is missing is coordination. Data Center Watch has begun tracking opposition groups and sharing data. AlgorithmWatch published a guide for European communities on how to resist data centers. But there is no equivalent resource for communities in the Global South, where the power imbalance between tech companies and local governments is even more severe.

The data center backlash is sometimes framed as NIMBYism, as people who simply do not want change in their backyard. But what these communities are asking for is basic: transparency about resource use, fair allocation of costs, and a say in decisions that reshape their lives. Those are not unreasonable demands. The fact that they have to fight this hard to get them says more about the industry than it does about the communities.

What comes next: The final phase of this ISR will synthesize all three phases into a cohesive story about what I have learned, what I think can be done, and what this means for how we think about "progress" in the age of AI.
