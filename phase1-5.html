The Invisible Workforce Behind AI

The Human Cost

How the AI industry exploits workers in the Global South and desperate job seekers everywhere

March 2026 · 15 min read

Phase 1 examined the environmental gap between Big Tech's promises and reality. But there is another form of exploitation powering the AI industry that receives far less attention: the people who build the data that makes AI work in the first place.

Every time you use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any other AI model, you are interacting with a system that was trained and refined by human beings. Not the researchers at OpenAI or Google, but hundreds of thousands of contract workers across the world, many earning less than $2 an hour, who label images, rate outputs, write training prompts, and review the most disturbing content the internet has to offer. The AI industry calls them "humans in the loop." Critics call it something else entirely.

Part I: The Supply Chain You Don't See

For an AI model to learn anything through supervised learning, it needs labeled data. Someone has to look at a photo and tag it as "cat." Someone has to read a paragraph and judge whether it is toxic or helpful. Someone has to review an AI-generated response and rank it against alternatives. This process, known as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), is what makes modern language models useful rather than incoherent.

The sheer scale of this work is enormous. The World Bank estimates that between 154 million and 435 million people are engaged in online gig work globally, constituting 4.4% to 12.5% of the global labor force. A significant and growing portion of this workforce is devoted to AI data work.

The companies that need this data (OpenAI, Meta, Google, Anthropic, Microsoft) almost never hire these workers directly. Instead, they contract with intermediary companies that recruit, manage, and pay the workers. The largest of these intermediaries is Scale AI, a San Francisco company valued at $13.8 billion, whose CEO Alexandr Wang became the world's youngest self-made billionaire on the backs of this labor model. In June 2025, Meta invested $14.3 billion into Scale AI for a 49% stake.

Scale AI operates through subsidiaries that most workers never realize are connected. Remotasks handles computer vision and autonomous vehicle data. Outlier handles generative AI annotation, primarily recruiting workers with advanced degrees to write prompts and evaluate outputs. Workers on Remotasks in Kenya did not know they were working for Scale AI at all, according to a 2023 investigation by The Verge. The relationship between these entities is deliberately obscured.

Part II: Remotasks and the Kenyan Exit

Kenya became one of the primary hubs for AI data work because it checked every box the industry needed: high English proficiency, a large population of educated but unemployed young people (youth unemployment approaches 67%), and, crucially, low wages. Tech companies and the Kenyan government alike promoted the country as a "Silicon Savannah," a destination where AI work would bring economic opportunity.

The reality was different. Workers on Remotasks reported earning less than $2 per hour, compared to $20 or more for equivalent work in the United States. Pay for some annotation tasks dropped below one cent per task due to what Oxford researchers described as "vicious competition" after the platform expanded to India and Venezuela. Late payments were described as "commonplace." A 2022 Oxford Internet Institute study found that Remotasks met the "minimum standards of fair work" in only one out of ten criteria.

Wage Theft by Design

Workers reported a consistent pattern: they would complete tasks, and when payday approached, their accounts would be locked with a message stating they had "violated a policy." No specifics were provided. No appeals process existed. The work was already done, the data already collected, and the workers received nothing. As one worker told 60 Minutes: "When it gets to the day before payday, they close the account and say that you violated a policy. And they don't pay you for the work you've done."

In March 2024, Remotasks abruptly shut down its operations in Kenya, locking thousands of workers out of their accounts with a last-minute email sent just hours before the exit. Workers who depended on the platform for their livelihoods were left stranded. Kenya's Data Labelers Association (DLA), an 800-member labor group based in Nairobi, is now weighing legal action.

The shutdown followed a pattern that Kenyan civil rights activist Nerima Wako-Ojiwa described precisely: "When we start to push back, in terms of protections of workers, a lot of these companies shut down and they move to a neighboring country." The AI supply chain, as Privacy International documented, "can be endlessly and instantly reconfigured, moving to wherever there is the right combination of skills, bandwidth, and wages."

"It's cheap labor. Like modern-day slavery." Nerima Wako-Ojiwa, Kenyan civil rights activist, on 60 Minutes (2024)

Part III: The Psychological Toll

Beyond the wages, there is the nature of the work itself. A significant portion of AI training involves content moderation: reviewing and labeling violent, sexually explicit, and otherwise traumatic material so that AI models learn to filter it out. The people who do this work are exposed to images and descriptions of murders, child sexual abuse, torture, and extreme violence for hours every day.

A 2025 survey by the research organization Equidem documented 60 independent incidents of psychological harm among just 76 workers in Colombia, Ghana, and Kenya. Workers reported anxiety, depression, irritability, panic attacks, PTSD, and substance dependence. A content moderator in Ghana reported reading up to 700 sexually explicit and violent texts per day, with the psychological toll causing him to lose his family.

In January 2025, several contractors sued Scale AI and Outlier, alleging psychological harm from being exposed to disturbing content. The lawsuit described workers developing "significant psychological distress and functional problems, including depression symptoms, anxiety, nightmares, and problems functioning in their work and relationships." Some images "appeared to depict real-life events" including "rapes, assaults on children, murders, and fatal car accidents."

Mental health support is either absent or inadequate. One BBC report found data labelers in Kenya who said they "felt their lives had ended" due to the content they were forced to review. The people absorbing this trauma are doing so in order for the rest of us to have a sanitized experience when we interact with AI tools.

Part IV: Outlier and the Freelancer Trap

While Remotasks targeted workers in the Global South, another Scale AI subsidiary, Outlier, has been targeting educated workers in the United States and Europe with a different but equally troubling model.

Outlier recruits people with graduate degrees and professional expertise to evaluate and improve AI outputs. The pitch is appealing: flexible hours, remote work, $15 to $50 per hour. Outlier blankets LinkedIn with sponsored recruitment messages and lists thousands of open positions on job boards like Glassdoor and Indeed.

The complaints are extensive and consistent. An Inc. Magazine investigation in November 2024, headlined "It's a Scam," documented widespread reports of non-payment, including workers completing lengthy unpaid onboarding and training processes only to be removed from projects without compensation. Workers who asked payment-related questions on Slack channels reported being removed from the company. Outlier's terms of service allow the company to withhold or delay payment, suspend accounts at any time without notice, and require binding arbitration that prevents class action lawsuits.

The Onboarding Trap

Multiple reports describe a pattern where Outlier requires workers to complete hours of unpaid "assessments" and "training" before gaining access to paid tasks. Some workers, particularly those applying for language-specific roles, reported being immediately rejected after completing assessments, fueling suspicion that the assessments themselves are being used as free training data. As one Quora reviewer from India wrote: "They will ask you to take the assessment and act as if it's very easy to earn money. However, once you've given the assessment, they will immediately reject you. They're basically using you to train their AI models for free."

Scale AI faces multiple lawsuits in San Francisco courts over alleged labor violations, underpaid wages, misclassification of workers, and the mass firing of 500 employees in August 2025 in violation of California labor law. Despite this, Scale AI's clients remain the most prominent names in the industry: OpenAI, Meta, Google, Microsoft, and the U.S. Department of Defense.

Part V: Mercor and Data Harvesting

A newer entrant in this space is Mercor, a San Francisco company that has raised over $100 million from investors including Peter Thiel and Jack Dorsey, with a reported valuation targeting $10 billion. Mercor describes itself as an AI-powered hiring platform that matches professionals with AI training and evaluation work.

The model is explicitly designed to extract domain knowledge from professionals in industries like finance, law, and medicine. Rather than negotiating data partnerships with companies, AI labs use Mercor to hire former employees of those companies, who then provide insights based on their real-world knowledge. In the company's own framing, this is a way to tap into "proprietary industry know-how" that incumbents refuse to share.

The criticism of Mercor centers on two issues. First, there are widespread reports that the platform posts job listings and conducts AI video interviews with no intention of hiring most applicants. A widely circulated Medium post from 2025 described applying for what appeared to be a legitimate role, only to discover "there was no actual job. Mercor has been posting positions across virtually every field, not to fill roles, but to collect vast amounts of interview data to train their AI systems." The company's one-way AI interviews record candidates' verbal responses, facial expressions, and behavioral patterns. Mercor states it will not use interview data to train AI models, but the volume of complaints suggests deep skepticism.

Second, for those who do get hired, the work is precarious. Mercor fired thousands of data labelers in 2025, only to rehire them for a similar project at considerably lower pay. Workers describe a "feast or famine" cycle where projects appear and disappear without warning. The company requires freelancers to install monitoring software that accesses cameras, microphones, and browser history, taking periodic screenshots throughout the workday.

Part VI: The Black Market

The pay disparity between Global South and Global North workers has created an entire shadow economy. An AlgorithmWatch investigation uncovered a black market where workers in Kenya buy and sell verified Outlier accounts registered in the United States, Canada, and European countries. Because Outlier pays workers according to regional rates, a verified European account can earn several times more than an African one for identical work.

Workers use residential proxies to disguise their true locations, bypassing the platform's fraud detection systems. WhatsApp groups openly advertise "verified Outlier accounts" that are "ready to task." The investigation traced the operation to individuals with Kenyan phone numbers who purchase identity verification from people in higher-paying regions.

This black market is a direct consequence of the pay structure: identical work, performed to identical quality standards, compensated at wildly different rates based solely on the worker's geography. The companies benefit from this arrangement because it keeps costs low in the Global South while maintaining a veneer of fair compensation in Western markets.

My Takeaway

What connects all of these stories is a deliberate architecture of invisibility. The workers do not know which AI models their labor trains. The end users of those models do not know the workers exist. The intermediary companies obscure their relationships with both the workers and the AI labs. And the AI labs themselves maintain plausible deniability by outsourcing the labor to third parties.

The Brookings Institution's October 2025 report on AI labor in the Global South concluded that none of the 15 major platforms it assessed scored better than "the bare minimum" on fair pay, conditions, contracts, management, and representation. Lawsuits are mounting, but as the Brookings researchers noted, "this whack-a-mole approach of filing lawsuits and investigations one at a time cannot effectively prevent structural labor abuses from occurring."

There is something deeply uncomfortable about the fact that the AI tools marketed as humanity's path to abundance are built on a labor model that a Kenyan civil rights activist compares to modern-day slavery, that a former video editor describes as "training AI to take my job someday," and that a researcher at the University of the Republic in Montevideo frames as the continuation of colonial resource extraction in digital form.

The next time someone tells you that AI will solve the world's problems, it is worth asking: for whom? And at whose expense?
