The Price of Progress

How AI infrastructure reshapes the places and people it touches

March 2026 · 16 min read

In Phase 1, I looked at the gap between what Big Tech promises and what their own reports reveal. The numbers were damning on their own: emissions climbing, water use surging, lobbying dollars flowing to defeat the very regulations these companies claim to support.

But numbers only tell part of the story. For Phase 2, I wanted to understand what this looks like on the ground, in real communities that are living through the arrival of a massive data center. I chose two places: Richland Parish, Louisiana, where Meta is building the largest data center in the Western Hemisphere, and Querétaro, Mexico, where Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are transforming a drought-stricken farming region into what local officials call "the data center valley."

Both stories share the same basic structure: a tech giant arrives with promises, a local government bends over backward to accommodate them, and the people who actually live there are left to figure out the consequences.

Case Study 1: Richland Parish, Louisiana

Richland Parish is a farming community of about 20,000 people in northeastern Louisiana. It is one of the poorest parishes in the state. The area is known for cotton farming, and for generations, the telltale sign of arriving in the parish was white tufts of cotton clinging to fences and ditches along the road. That has changed.

In December 2024, Meta announced it would build a $10 billion data center on 2,250 acres of former farmland between the towns of Rayville and Delhi. The facility, codenamed "Hyperion," will be Meta's largest anywhere in the world, covering roughly 4 million square feet. To put that in perspective, Mark Zuckerberg posted a comparison showing the campus overlaid on Manhattan. The post received over 50 million impressions.

$10B
Meta's investment in the Richland Parish Hyperion data center, the largest single private-sector investment in Louisiana's history
Meta, Dec 2025

The promises were enormous. Meta projected 500+ direct jobs with salaries at least 150% of the state average, over 1,000 indirect jobs, $875 million in contracts with Louisiana businesses in the first year alone, $200 million in local infrastructure improvements, and community grants to local schools and nonprofits. Louisiana's Economic Development Secretary called it a project that would "revitalize one of our state's beautiful rural areas."

But there is another side to the story that takes more effort to find.

The Energy Question

Hyperion will consume more electricity in a single day than the entire city of New Orleans at peak summer demand. To power it, Entergy Louisiana is building three new natural gas-fired power plants. The total cost of this new energy infrastructure exceeds $3 billion. Meta will pay the first half of a 30-year loan for the plants, but the remainder of the cost, plus $550 million for a new transmission line, will be covered by regional ratepayers according to reporting from Wired and the Louisiana Illuminator.

Who Pays for the Power?

Meta and Entergy have claimed that the data center's electricity payments will cut customer costs and storm charges by about 10%, resulting in $650 million in savings over 15 years. But watchdog organizations and energy analysts have pointed out that this framing obscures the fact that residential customers may absorb significant infrastructure costs. The Sierra Club noted that the "full cost" Meta is paying is "tenuously defined," and that the long-term rate impacts remain unclear until the facility is actually operational, which is not expected until 2030.

Louisiana rewrote zoning laws and signed a sales tax exemption for data centers built before 2029 specifically to land this project. The state's governor, Jeff Landry, worked behind closed doors with Meta beginning in early 2024, well before any public announcement. Competitive bidding requirements were suspended. The deal was fast-tracked at every level.

What's Happening on the Ground

The changes in Richland Parish have arrived faster than anyone or any zoning plan could absorb. Delhi mayor Jesse Washington described it bluntly: the project is "bringing more workers than we have residents, and more trucks than we can count." By mid-2025, over 600 construction workers were already on site. At peak construction, Meta projects 5,000 to 6,000 workers. Delhi has a population of 2,600.

Housing Displacement

Since the announcement, land prices in the parish have doubled. More than a dozen low-income families were evicted from a trailer park whose owners are building housing for incoming Meta construction workers. Both Black and white families in trailer home parks have been displaced so higher-earning workers could move in. Mayor Washington confirmed these evictions, and a Capital B investigation documented the pattern in detail.

Infrastructure Strain

Construction traffic has led to accidents on roads connecting the parish, some of them fatal, according to Delhi's mayor. "We're working every day on new sewer and water lines," Washington said. The parish's existing infrastructure was built for a small farming community, not for the largest private construction project in the state's history. While Meta has committed $200 million toward road and water system improvements, residents are living through the disruption now while the benefits remain years away.

Water Concerns

The data center will require up to 5 million gallons of water per day for cooling. The site draws from the Mississippi River Alluvial Aquifer. While some local farmers are confident the aquifer will replenish fast enough, environmental researchers from Virginia Tech have warned that the surge in demand from rural data centers could strain the infrastructure needed to distribute water to residents. Meta has committed to "restoring 100% of water consumption" to local watersheds, but the mechanism for doing so is a partnership with an environmental restoration firm, not a direct return of water to the taps of Richland Parish.

The story of Richland Parish is still unfolding. The data center will not be operational until 2030, and some of the promised benefits may eventually materialize. But the pattern is already clear: a community with limited resources and limited bargaining power is absorbing the full weight of a $10 billion construction project while the company and the state celebrate the investment on social media.

A Brookings Institution report from February 2026 put it plainly: the standard model of data center development produces mostly short-term construction jobs and relatively little long-term, high-value employment. A project that generates 5,000 construction jobs may sustain only a few hundred permanent positions once built.

Case Study 2: Querétaro, Mexico

If Richland Parish shows what happens when a single company overwhelms a small community, Querétaro shows what happens when an entire industry descends on a region already in crisis.

Since 2020, the central Mexican state of Querétaro has attracted over $12 billion in data center investment from Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and others. The state is now home to at least 14 operational facilities, with permits issued for at least 20. Most are concentrated in two small municipalities: Colón and El Marqués, both located near the state capital. Governor Mauricio Kuri has actively courted these investments, and Mexico's President Claudia Sheinbaum has promoted data center construction as essential for the country "to advance in artificial intelligence."

The problem is that Colón is a semi-arid municipality of about 65,000 people that has been suffering from drought for over two years. From April to July 2024, the drought was classified as "exceptional," the highest level of water scarcity. Dams are nearly empty. Farmers sell whatever scarce produce survives. Residents in some communities get water from the tap only three days a week.

Share of Colón's public water allocation claimed by Microsoft's water concession for just one of its two data center units
Thomson Reuters Foundation, Sep 2024

Microsoft obtained a concession to extract 25 million liters of water per year from the Valle de San Juan del Río aquifer for a single data center unit. That aquifer already has a deficit of 56.8 billion liters according to Mexico's National Water Commission (CONAGUA). When the state's Secretary for Sustainable Development was asked how much water was being allocated to all 20 data centers, he said he did not "have the data because it is not in my power to request it," and compared their consumption to what a restaurant uses in a month.

Neither the companies building data centers nor the state government would disclose specific water usage figures to journalists. This opacity is a recurring theme across every data center case study I have looked at.

Broken Promises

In 2022, UN-Habitat announced that Microsoft would invest $3.9 million in 21 community projects in Colón and El Marqués to "improve the quality of life of the communities near the data centres." The projects included improvements to parks, outdoor gyms, soccer fields, and public spaces.

19 of 21 Projects Incomplete

When journalists from the Thomson Reuters Foundation visited the sites for all 21 projects in late 2025, they found that 19 remained unfinished. In La Esperanza, a Microsoft data center sits next to an abandoned outdoor gym and an unkempt soccer field that had been slated for improvement. A local coach reported that the field has been forgotten and no improvements have been made despite the promises. The accompanying needs assessment report from Microsoft's program did not even recommend investments to address the drought, which was identified as the community's primary challenge.

The jobs picture is similarly thin. While the CloudHQ project announced in September 2025 claims it will provide 900 permanent jobs, figures from existing operations tell a different story. One specific data center facility in Mexico employed only 64 people as of 2025. Construction phases may bring 2,200 temporary positions, but permanent roles are often limited to security and cleaning staff.

"Digital Extractivism"

Researchers have described what is happening in Querétaro as a form of "digital extractivism," a term that draws a parallel to the extraction of minerals and raw materials from the Global South to fuel economies elsewhere. The data centers serve global AI training workloads, but the water, energy, and land costs are borne locally.

Sandra García, a 38-year-old factory worker in Colón, opens her faucet and no water comes out. This has become normal. Meanwhile, data center campuses nearby are built with landscaping and filtered water. María Selva Ortiz of Friends of the Earth in Uruguay captured the sentiment shared across Latin America when she said: "We feel that foreign multinationals come to use our natural resources with no benefit to us."

Mexico's federal environmental agency has yet to establish water-use caps for data centers. There is no specific legislation governing their resource consumption. In Chile, environmental courts intervened to force Google to switch cooling methods after community opposition, but in Mexico and many other countries, local governments lack the technical capacity to challenge corporate permits.

The Patterns

Looking at these two case studies side by side, several patterns emerge that extend well beyond Richland Parish and Querétaro.

Opacity by design. In both cases, the scale of resource consumption was either not disclosed or actively obscured. In Louisiana, the full cost allocation between Meta and ratepayers remains unclear. In Mexico, the state government could not or would not say how much water was allocated to data centers. NDAs between developers and local officials are widespread. A review of 31 Virginia municipalities with existing or proposed data centers found that 80% had NDAs in place.

Speed over scrutiny. Both projects were fast-tracked through regulatory processes designed to attract investment, not to protect communities. Louisiana rewrote zoning laws and suspended competitive bidding. Mexico has no specific legislation governing data center resource use. The federal government in the U.S. has similarly moved to streamline permitting by using categorical exclusions from the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), reducing opportunities for local input.

Construction boom, employment bust. The Brookings Institution documented a consistent pattern: data center projects that create thousands of construction jobs sustain only a few hundred permanent positions. Richland Parish may see 5,000 workers at peak construction but perhaps 500 permanent jobs. In Mexico, one facility employs 64 people. The economic benefit falls off a cliff once the center is built.

Costs are local, benefits are global. The AI training workloads running inside these facilities serve a global market. The water depletion, noise, traffic, displaced families, and strained infrastructure remain in the community. A Carnegie Mellon study from July 2025 projected that data center growth could increase electricity bills 8% nationally and as much as 25% in some regional markets.

My Takeaway

Before starting this research, I understood conceptually that data centers had local impacts. What I did not fully appreciate was how deliberate the information asymmetry is. These are not accidental oversights. The opacity around water use, the NDAs with local officials, the fast-tracked permits, the framing of infrastructure costs as "customer savings" rather than cost-shifting: all of it functions as a system designed to move projects forward before communities can organize a response.

What struck me most about Richland Parish was the evictions. Families displaced from trailer parks so construction workers could move in. That is not a side effect of progress. That is a direct transfer from the poorest residents of one of the poorest parishes in Louisiana to the workforce of one of the most valuable companies on earth. And in Querétaro, the image of a Microsoft data center sitting next to an abandoned soccer field that Microsoft itself promised to fix says more than any sustainability report ever could.

These stories are not unique. They are playing out in dozens of communities across the United States and around the world. The next phase of this research will look at what people are doing about it.
